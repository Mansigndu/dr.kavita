Answers To Common Kidney Health Questions

Get quick answers to some of the most commonly asked questions regarding kidney disease, blood pressure, dialysis, and nephrology consultations.

When should I see a nephrologist?

You should see a nephrologist if you have kidney disease, persistent protein in urine, blood in urine, high creatinine, uncontrolled high blood pressure, recurrent kidney stones, or diabetes affecting your kidneys.

Does a high creatinine level always mean I need dialysis?

No. A high creatinine level does not automatically mean dialysis is needed. The cause, kidney function, symptoms, and overall clinical picture are more important than a single number.

Can kidney disease be treated without dialysis?

Yes. Many patients with kidney disease never need dialysis. Early diagnosis, blood pressure control, diabetes management, medications, and dietary changes can help slow or even stabilize kidney disease.

What are the early signs of kidney disease?

Early kidney disease often has no symptoms. Some people may notice swelling, frothy urine, high blood pressure, tiredness, or changes in urination, but many patients feel completely well.

What foods should I avoid if I have kidney disease?

There is no single kidney diet that suits everyone. Dietary advice depends on your kidney function, potassium, phosphorus, sodium, and protein needs. A personalized diet plan from a renal dietitian is often the best approach.

Can kidney disease be reversed?

Some causes of kidney injury are reversible if treated early. Chronic kidney disease is usually not reversible, but its progression can often be slowed significantly with the right treatment.

How often should I check my kidney function?

The frequency depends on your condition. Some patients need testing every few months, while others may only require annual monitoring. Your nephrologist will advise the appropriate schedule.

Is kidney disease hereditary?

Some kidney diseases, such as polycystic kidney disease, can run in families. Most kidney diseases, however, are caused by conditions like diabetes, high blood pressure, autoimmune diseases, or infections.

Can I prevent kidney disease?

In many cases, yes. Keeping blood pressure and diabetes under control, avoiding unnecessary painkillers, staying hydrated, maintaining a healthy weight, and having regular health check-ups can reduce your risk.

Can I exercise if I have kidney disease?

Yes. Most patients with kidney disease are encouraged to stay physically active. Your exercise plan should be tailored to your overall health and kidney condition.

Do I need to stop eating protein if I have kidney disease?

No. Protein should not be eliminated. Most patients need the right amount of high-quality protein—not too much and not too little. Your protein requirement depends on your stage of kidney disease and should be individualized.
